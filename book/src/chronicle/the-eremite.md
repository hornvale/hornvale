# The Eremite

For as long as the world has been peopled, it kept a hidden equation: to have a
mind was to be a people, and to be a people was to settle and to speak. The four
goblinoid kinds each carried a psychology, a way of perceiving, and a tongue —
all together, or not at all — and everything else was fauna, a body without an
inner life. The equation was never written as law. It lived in a single
load-bearing check, and in a hundred small places that read it as gospel: *if a
thing has a mind, it is one of the peoples.*

A dragon breaks the equation. It is as cunning and proud as anything that walks,
and in the old stories it speaks — yet it raises no village, keeps no company,
answers to no one. To give it the mind it deserves, the model demanded it become
a settler, which it is not. The line the model had drawn was never between the
thinking and the mindless; it was between the communal and the alone, and it had
mistaken the one for the other.

This campaign dissolves that line. Social organization becomes a dimension every
creature carries — sessile, solitary, gregarious, settled — set apart from the
*capacities* of mind, sense, and speech. The welded bundle becomes a nested
order: to speak, a thing must perceive; to perceive, it must have a mind; and
only the settled carry all three. A creature may now hold a mind and nothing
more. The three chromatic dragons are given exactly that — a solitary
temperament, a will that stands where others flee, an "us" drawn no wider than
the self, a patience counted in centuries — and no tongue at all, for the
language of a creature with no one to speak to is a later country.

The dissolution cost more than it promised. The old equation had seeped
everywhere: into the pass that inscribes each people's mind onto the world's
record; the pages that render every speaker's dictionary and phonology; the
tallies that sum the peoples' claim on the land. Each had encoded *a mind means a
people* as a silent premise — and each had to be taught, one failure at a time,
the difference between a mind and a settlement, between a mind and a voice. Even
the dragons' own kinship betrayed it: the three share one ancestral family, and a
routine that had always found that family empty of speakers suddenly found it
full of them, and reached for words that were never there. The reckoning was the
true measure of how deep a false identity runs once a model has lived inside it
long enough.

And when it was done, nothing moved. Not one byte of any world changed, because a
dragon walks no placed roster yet: its new mind waits in the registry, authored
and correct and unread, the way a coin is struck long before it is ever spent.
The eremite thinks now — in the dark, alone — and the world has not yet noticed.
That noticing is the next campaign.
