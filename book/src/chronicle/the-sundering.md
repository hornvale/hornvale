# The Sundering

*The routes were always there; this campaign lets the sea move over them.*

The Connection Graph read the world's transport topology off the map and made
it legible, but left the deep-history bake untouched — displacement still walked
raw cell-adjacency, stepping across open ocean as if it were meadow. The
Sundering, the second slice of the connection-graph campaign, makes the bake
*follow* that topology. And it does so with a single, load-bearing correction:
the graph is not one fixed map but a **function of the era**. The sea moves.

## The moving sea

A cell is ocean in a given era if its elevation lies below that era's sea
level — and the sea level is not a constant. The paleoclimate already carried
it: each era's eustatic sea level, present height plus the change the ice
volume drives, falling toward a glacial low-stand. When the ice advances, the
sea falls and the drowned shelf between a mainland and an island rises into a
**land bridge**; when the ice retreats, the sea rises and the bridge drowns.
The bake derives one connection graph per era over this moving coastline, and
its three migration paths — a community fleeing a frozen home, a raider
reaching a neighbour, a daughter settling new ground — all walk the graph *of
the era in force*, crossing only where the land and sea of that moment permit.

This is the one substrate that had stood outside the simulation's own
principle. Temperature, habitability, ice: all are functions over space and
time. The connection graph alone had been a function of space only. The
Sundering conforms it — `graph(era)` — and in doing so earns both halves of its
name. Where the low sea opens a bridge, peoples cross; where the rising sea
drowns one, the peoples who crossed are *sundered*, cut off on their landmass
to drift apart. Isolation, which the Connection Graph could only draw, now
shapes who ends up where.

## What the world turned out to be

The graph works, and works richly: on the seed-42 world, eleven of twelve
climate migrations cross water that exists only in the era they moved, and the
glacial low-stand reconnects the great majority of the refugia the ice drives
peoples toward. Isolation genuinely diverges the peoples — the world resolves
into four sea-bound landmasses over four peoples, and three of the four host
only a *subset* of them: a people that could not cross to a shore is simply
absent from it. This is the payoff, and it is structural, measured over the
occupation record itself, invented nowhere.

But the diaspora that travels these routes is honest and modest — a dozen
climate displacements across two millennia, not the larger number the old
ocean-walking bake reported. That larger number was an artifact: a migration
that strode across open water counts crossings the sea should forbid. Removing
it is most of the drop. What remains is a quieter, truer picture, and the
reason it is quiet is worth stating plainly, because the world stated it, not
the design. Peoples settle the warmest, most fertile land — which is also the
land the ice least disturbs. A glacial that buries the empty cold cells leaves
the warm settled ones habitable, and the peoples on them ride it out. The
displacement that *does* fire is the temperate edge, the peoples who happened
to settle where the ice reaches. And crowding, the other engine of migration,
is absent entirely: the world has vacant land to spare, so no people is ever
pressed off its ground by another.

So the sundering sea is the correct substrate, and the volume of the diaspora
that rides it is a question this campaign does not answer — it is a question of
*pressure*, of peoples crowding one another off good land, which is the explicit
subject of the next campaign in the program. The Sundering lays the routes. What
fills them is conflict's to build.
