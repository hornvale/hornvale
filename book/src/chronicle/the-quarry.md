# The Quarry

Every hazard a creature had learned to fear was, until now, a thing of the world —
the uncanny of a cursed place, the killing heat, the killing cold, all sourced from
climate and stone. But the oldest dread of all is not of the world; it is of other
creatures, of being *eaten*. This campaign added the first **biotic** hazard — a
field of predator presence — so a creature can shun the ground where carnivores
range, and took the first step toward the food web.

The design fell out of a negation that revealed a duality. Strip a predator-hazard
to its structure and its opposite is not "no predator" but the *same predator read
with the sign reversed*: a carnivore field is a quarry's **threat** — flee it — and,
to a predator, its **food** — be drawn to it. One field, two signs, the eater and
the eaten reading the same ground in opposite directions. It is the negative charge
The Mettle and The Bane both left on their far shores — the fear that becomes
desire — and it waits there still; this campaign built only the threat side, the
quarry's flight. The visible hunting predator that stalks, and the slow tides of
predator and prey populations rising and falling against each other, are the rest
of the trophic engine, reserved.

The field itself is the honest part. A first instinct would read a cell's carrying
capacity for carnivores — but capacity is broad, and a creature's fear should not
light up every fertile acre a predator *could* live in. What matters is where
predators *actually are*, and the coexistence stack already answers this: a
realized density, thinned by competition where peoples hold the land and spread
thin across an apex's wide range, so it concentrates on genuine wild territory and
leaves settled ground quiet. And the dread of it derives, as every dread now does,
from what a creature is — from its own diet. A creature's carnivory sets both what
it eats and how much it fears being eaten: a herbivore fears the predator fully, an
omnivore half, and an obligate apex not at all, for it *is* the thing others flee.
The same number, the weight on animal prey, reads forward as appetite and backward
as terror.

The peoples who walk the world today, though, are bold and omnivorous, and they
shrug the ambient risk off. The predator field is *latent* — a background wariness,
not the acute panic of a hunter at one's heels — and scaled so that a goblin or a
bugbear, half-carnivore and unafraid, never crosses into flight over the mere
presence of distant carnivores; their world is byte-for-byte unchanged, the
possessed hobgoblin's daily rounds unaltered. The fear is wired all the same,
derived and correct, and it waits — as The Bane's dragon-niches wait for their
dragons — for the day a vulnerable creature opens its eyes as an agent: a timid
herbivore, a small and undefended thing, which will feel the carnivore ground for
what it is and give it a wide berth. The quarry's fear is built. It sleeps in the
bold, and will wake in the meek.
