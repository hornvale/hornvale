# The Phantom

The Haunt gave fear a memory, but a memory only of permanent things. The ground it
taught a creature to shun was ground still dangerous — a static hazard in the terrain,
avoided because it is, sensibly, to be avoided. The Alarm gave fear a different reach:
a herd's panic, spreading cell to cell, rising when a neighbour bolts and gone the next
moment. That fear was real while it lasted and then it was nothing. This campaign lets a
creature remember it anyway — and so go on avoiding ground where terror once was, long
after the terror has passed and the ground is safe again. It is the first fear of
nothing: the phantom, and with it the small beginning of superstition.

The difficulty was that the alarm leaves no trace. It is never written to the ledger; it
is computed fresh each moment from where the frightened creatures happen to be standing,
and then it is gone. The Haunt could read the terrain of the past because the terrain of
the past is the terrain of the present — rock does not move. But the alarm of a Tuesday
is not recoverable on Friday by looking at Friday. What saved the design is that it is
recoverable by looking at *Tuesday*: the alarm is a pure function of who stood where and
who was afraid, and all of that is in the record. So the memory does not store the fright;
it *re-derives* it, reconstructing the long-gone alarm at each place the creature once
stood, from the frozen account of that day. Nothing is committed, no new kind of fact is
minted; the belief remains, as every belief in this world remains, a reading of the past
rather than a note kept about it.

This is the point at which fear becomes fallible, and it becomes fallible in a precise
way. The memory itself is true: the creature genuinely was frightened there, on that day,
and the reconstruction is faithful to what happened. What is false is the silent inference
the memory invites — *therefore it is still dangerous* — when the danger has in fact moved
on. A true memory and a mistaken conclusion: that is the exact anatomy of a phobia, and of
the superstition it seeds, for the creature cannot reason its way clear. It does not
remember *that a herd-mate panicked beside it*; it remembers only *that here was terror*,
the cause already abstracted away, leaving a dread attached to a place for no reason the
creature can name or examine. Only experience can dislodge it. The rule The Haunt wrote
but never needed — that a place is feared only if the *last* visit there was frightening —
finally does its work: stand safely on the haunted ground and the fear lifts, disproven.
Until then the creature routes its journeys around a danger that is not there.

There was a price, and finding the right way to pay it taught the campaign its sharpest
lesson. Reconstructing the alarm of every past day, for every creature, at read time, is
expensive — and in a long-running world thick with wary beasts it grew ruinous, the same
arbitration recomputed a thousand times over. The instinct was to make the reconstruction
cheaper. The truer diagnosis, once the problem was held up against how memory works
elsewhere — a ledger's materialized view, a brain's consolidated trace, a sentinel's
logged case — was that the simulation *already computes* each creature's fright the moment
it happens, to decide how the creature moves, and then throws that answer away only to
reconstruct it later at great cost. The fix was not to reconstruct faster but to stop
discarding: remember each verdict once, keyed to the day, and — because a past day's fright
can never change as the world moves forward — keep it forever without fear of it going
stale. A memo of a fact that cannot rot. With that, and with the observation that ground
already frightening for its own terrain need never have its ghosts recomputed at all, the
cost fell back to where it belonged, no worse than the world already pays to remember where
the water is.

The peoples, sat on their good ground, are never frightened, and so carry no phantoms; the
world of seed 42 is unchanged to the byte. The phantom stirs only where a real terror once
swept through and a creature was there to feel it — the wild thing that shies, ever after,
from the safe clearing where the herd once broke and ran. Fear that spreads, fear that is
remembered as a place, and now fear that outlives the danger that caused it: the creatures
of this world have been given, one campaign at a time, the beginnings of an inner life that
does not always tell them the truth.
