# The Consonance

*Two moons, thirty days and sixty. A culture that has watched both long
enough to know their periods now notices they line up — and reaches for
whatever it already believes about bonds, journeys, and balance to say
why.*

Every explained fact this project renders so far stands alone: a moon
count, a day's length, each landing in a culture's account as its own
sentence, drawn from its own witnessed value. This campaign adds the
first fact that is *about* two other facts at once — a small-integer
relationship between two moons' own periods, when one happens to sit
close enough to a clean ratio (2:1, 3:2, and eight other low-order
rationals) to be worth remarking on. Most worlds' moons don't line up
this cleanly; when they do, exactly one relationship surfaces per world,
the single best match among every pair a world's moons offer.

Seeing the ratio and being *equipped* to witness it are different
thresholds. A culture needs the same sky-reading depth this project
already grades observability by, pushed higher than plain moon-counting
demands — noticing a relationship between two cycles takes more sustained
watching than noticing either cycle alone. Below that threshold, nothing
is silently omitted: the culture's account carries a present, explicit
record that this lies beyond what their own sky-craft can reach, the same
honest shape every other beyond-capability gap in this project already
takes.

Once witnessed, the ratio doesn't get a bespoke story. It flows into the
same weighted causal-schema library every other explained fact already
draws from — a culture's own subsistence and sociality tilting the odds
toward explaining the relationship as two things bound by a bond, two
things walking the same road, or two things holding each other in
balance, exactly as those same schemas already explain why a day is the
length it is. No new machinery, no new schema built for this occasion —
the library that already existed turned out to fit.

Whether that relationship can be *said* precisely is its own question,
gated by the numeral-system rung Few and Many gave every tongue: a
culture whose counting stops at the near-universal subitizing floor can
still notice "the two moons keep step," but only a tongue that counts
past that floor can say "two turns of one for every turn of the other" —
the same rung already governing how much of a spoken number a listener
retains now also governs whether a *relationship* between two numbers is
nameable at all, proven as a standing capability rather than wired into
any character's spoken account.
