# Few and Many

*Two listeners hear the same sentence. One keeps "about 1.5." The other
keeps "more than one."*

Every filter this project has built so far sits on the speaker's side: a
culture's account of the world passes through what it holds, what it
could have witnessed, how it carves what it cannot classify, what it
values — four stages, all upstream of the sentence a listener actually
hears. Once a sentence is spoken, every listener has understood it
identically, no matter who they are. This campaign builds the first filter
on the other side of that exchange.

Every tongue now draws its own numeral-system rung — how far its counting
words reach past the near-universal floor every creature already shares
(rapid, exact recognition of about one to four items, the same capacity
subitizing names in real cognitive science, not something any culture
lacks). Most tongues count fully, the typologically ordinary case; a few
build no counting words beyond that floor at all, and a few build all the
way to measured decimals. This is not a claim about how clever a people
is — it mirrors, deliberately, how this project already draws whether a
tongue marks evidence or noun class morphologically: historically and
environmentally contingent, drawn, never derived from a species' mind.

The mechanism is one function doing two jobs. The same code that would
render a quantity at a given rung also tells you what a listener capped at
that rung retains when someone else says a number more precisely than
they can hold. Told "its day lasts about 1.5 standard days," a
full-counting listener keeps "two" — the nearest whole count. A listener
whose tongue never built counting past the floor keeps "more than one" —
not a wrong number, just as much of the truth as their own language gives
them a way to hold. Nothing is invented to make this a two-function
system; retention and rendering are the same capacity, read from two
directions.

This campaign proves the mechanism against the Book's own existing
day-length sentences — the same ones every world already renders — without
yet changing what any of them say aloud. A speaker still says exactly what
they said before this campaign; only a listener's own retention is new,
and only as a standing, provable capability, not yet wired into how a
character in the world actually comes to know something they're told.
That wiring — a possessed character's own numeracy gating what sinks in
when they're told a fact, rather than always retaining the sentence's full
surface precision — is the obvious next turn of this thread, deliberately
left for whoever takes it: the mechanism is real and proven; giving it a
voice in a lived exchange is a different day's work.
