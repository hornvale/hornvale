# The Compound Word

*Not every word is spoken from one root.*

[The Single Saying](./the-single-saying.md) gave every crate that draws a
deterministic stream one authored place to say its labels — a constant
and its manifest entry, fused, so the two could no longer drift apart. It
left one crate out: `domains/history`, which arrived mid-campaign from
work already in flight elsewhere, and whose labels didn't fit either
shape the fusion knew. Some of its names are children of one root —
`residue` and `structures`, drawn relative to a seed the crate is handed,
the same way a branch is named relative to the tree it grows from. Others
are not children of anything in this crate at all: the deep-history
bake's own labels are rooted at a different place entirely, a different
part of the world that merely documents its words here because this is
where its family of words belongs, not where they are spoken from.

A saying can hold both at once. The fix was not to force one shape onto
the other, but to let one authored list say a root, its children, and its
independent cousins together — and still generate, from that single
saying, the one manifest entry each of them needs. Nothing about how any
of these labels are actually drawn changed; only where they are written
down, and what they are written down as.

That "what" turned out to matter more than expected. Two of the crate's
own labels — `residue` and `structures` — had always been published
under their bare names, unqualified by the root they in fact belong to.
Every other crate with a root and children had long since learned to
name a child by its full lineage: not `star-mass` alone, but
`astronomy/star-mass`. This one crate, born before that convention
existed, had never been asked to match it. Making it speak in one
saying meant making it speak consistently — the two labels now carry
their family name, the same as every sibling crate's already do. A name
was not wrong before; it simply hadn't yet been asked to say who its
parent was.
