# The Alarm

The Dread taught a creature to fear a hazard, The Bane taught it to fear each kind
of hazard differently, and The Mettle taught it how *much* to fear. All three fears
had one thing in common: they were the creature's own. A thing stood on dangerous
ground, read the danger with its own senses, and fled. This campaign builds the
first fear a creature does not sense for itself — the fear it *catches* from
another. A herbivore at the edge of a herd sees its neighbour bolt and bolts too,
though it never saw the wolf; the alarm runs through the herd faster than any of
them could have found the danger alone.

The temptation is to build this as perception: let a creature look at its
neighbours, read their distress, and respond. But the world forbids it, and the
prohibition is old and deliberate. Every creature in a tick is simulated alone
against a frozen ledger — it cannot see another creature's feelings, because
feelings are never written down; affect is immaterial, a pure reading of a
decision, gone the instant it is computed. And even if it could, a creature reading
its neighbours *as it moves* would make the outcome depend on the order the
creatures happened to be visited — the one thing a deterministic world cannot
allow. The Belonging met this wall first and chose to stand a creature's longing for
company on a *field* — its distance from home — rather than on the sight of any
actual companion. The Alarm makes the same choice for the same reason: fear travels
not as a glance between agents but as a **field over the land**.

At the start of each tick, before any creature moves, the world takes a census of
dread. It asks each creature, reading only the frozen past, whether its own Danger
drive is lit — whether it stands on genuinely fearful ground. Every creature that is
stamps its terror onto the cell it occupies and the cells one step away: an **alarm
field**, a map of where fear currently is. Then the creatures move, and the Danger
drive each of them already carries reads this field exactly as it reads the land's
own hazards — one more term added to the threat it feels, before its boldness
decides how much of that threat to believe. Fear-contagion turned out to be no new
drive at all, but the *same* drive with a *second source*: where danger once flowed
only from the ground, it now flows also from the frightened. A creature made afraid
by the terrain and a creature made afraid by its neighbour's fear flee by the
identical machinery; only the origin of the dread differs.

The census-before-movement is not an accident of implementation but the load-bearing
choice, and it is what keeps the herd from madness. Because the alarm field is built
from the frozen past — before anyone has borrowed anyone's fear — the only creatures
who can *emit* alarm are those afraid of something real. Borrowed fear never
re-emits. Were it otherwise, a single startle would feed on itself: neighbour
frightening neighbour frightening neighbour, a stampede outliving by hours the wolf
that started it, a panic with no predator. That failure has a name in epidemiology —
a contagion whose every case spawns more than one — and the world sidesteps it not
by tuning a damping constant but by construction. Alarm is a *halo* around true
danger. Remove the danger and, on the very next tick, the halo is gone and the herd
settles. A creature at the edge of the halo bolts one step clear of it and, finding
the fear does not follow, stops. The wave reaches exactly one rank past the danger
and no further — the cautious, terminating seed of a thing that could later be made
to ripple across a whole herd, once its ripples can be proven to die.

Like every fear before it, this one sleeps in the worlds that already exist. The
alarm term only ever *adds* to a creature's dread, and the settled peoples of a
generated world are calm, sat on their good ground by The Confluence — none of them
is ever afraid enough of the terrain to raise the alarm, so the field they walk
through is empty, and seed 42's every committed byte is what it was. The contagion
wakes only where genuine terror already burns and a calmer creature stands close
enough to catch it: the wild herd at the margin of predator country, the timid thing
beside the cornered one. The far shores wait in reserve, as they always do — the
self-sustaining wave that crosses a herd, its opposite pole where a calm creature
*quiets* its neighbours rather than frightening them, the per-kind susceptibility
that would let a skittish deer catch panic a lone predator shrugs off. The Alarm
builds only the first, surest thing: that fear, like the danger it answers, is
something the land can hold and pass from one creature to the next.
