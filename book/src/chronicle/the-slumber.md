# The Slumber

Every creature in Hornvale carried a rhythm it never kept. A species' activity
cycle — diurnal, nocturnal, crepuscular — was authored at genesis and then
ignored: creatures foraged, drank, and sought comfort around the clock, tireless
and sleepless. [The Temperament](./the-temperament.md) had given them drives to
resolve; this campaign gave them a *day*. It added the third drive, fatigue, and
in doing so woke the dormant cycle — a creature now tires, sleeps, and seeks its
other needs only while awake.

The shape came from chronobiology's two-process model. Sleep is the interaction
of a homeostatic pressure — a sleep debt that accrues while awake and discharges
while asleep — and a circadian oscillator that predicts when conditions are
good. Fatigue is the first: a stock drive, the structural twin of thirst, folding
over a creature's committed rests. The wake cycle is the second, and a pass
through ideonomy lifted it into something larger than sleep. Strip the sun from
"circadian" and what remains is *an entrainable oscillator gating behaviour* —
three numbers, a time-cue, a period, and how far it free-runs without the cue.
That generalizes past sleep to any periodically-timed behaviour, and past
biology to any creature: swap the cue from the sun to the moons and you have a
lycanthrope; to a place and you have an undead walking its death-hour; to nothing
and you have a construct on a programmed cycle. The wake clock The Slumber ships
is the first instance of that engine — sun, circadian, entrained — the way the
metabolism gate before it was the first instance of a per-kind datum steering
behaviour.

What actually shipped is the coarse tier of that oscillator: a fractional-day
window keyed to the activity cycle, active by day or by night. It is deliberately
the first rung — the true solar terminator, with its polar summers and its
seasons drifting the dusk, is the reserved refinement, a body-swap of one
function against astronomy that already computes the sun's altitude at any
latitude. Coarse constrains fine: "awake with the daylight" is right nearly
always; the real sun only sharpens the edges.

The interesting work was where the wake cycle collided with the rest of the mind,
and each collision moved the design. A fatigue steep enough to force a *daily*
rest left creatures perpetually weary, their arousal never settling — so fatigue
became a gentle sleep *debt*, and the daily rest came from the cycle, not the
drive. A creature tethered to its home to sleep could never explore far enough to
discover water — so a creature sleeps *where it is*, bedding down in the field, and
a stranded one is never blocked from rest. And the population-health metric,
which sampled each creature's felt state at midnight, found everyone asleep and
blind to their waking distress — so it learned to read them mid-morning, while
they are up and before the noon heat, because distress is a waking state and a
creature hot only at high noon is not chronically suffering.

From all of this the rhythm falls out on its own. A diurnal creature wakes,
forages while its thirst and its comfort drive it, lets its sleep debt rise
gently, and at nightfall — its needs gated silent — beds down and sleeps through
to dawn, when it rises and begins again. A creature dying of thirst wakes to
drink regardless; the deathless, having no metabolism, never sleep at all. The
engine beneath it — the oscillator stack that will one day give jet lag to
travellers, drift to creatures lost in lightless caves, and a moon-waxed potency
to things that were never diurnal — waits in the registry, its first instance
already keeping time.
