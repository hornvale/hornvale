# The Solitary Tongue

The Eremite gave the dragon a mind and left it mute, on the promise that the
language of a creature with no one to speak to was a country for another day.
This is that country. It turns out to be a strange one, and the strangeness is
the point.

A language changes when it is handed down. Every time a child learns a word from
a parent, a little of the sound can shift — a hardened consonant, a fronted
vowel — and over enough hand-offs the word drifts far from the form its
ancestors spoke. A people crowded into settlements, generation stacked on
generation, hands its tongue down constantly, and so its speech drifts fast and
splits into dialects that no longer quite understand each other. This is the
common case, and until now it was the *only* case: every speaking kind drifted
at the same rate, because the model counted rules, not hand-offs.

But a dragon hands nothing down. It broods alone in the dark for a thousand
years, and in all that time there is no child to teach, no parent to correct, no
generation to succeed it — no transmission at all. Its tongue is not passed on;
it is simply *kept*, held unchanging in one long memory. So where a people's
language drifts, a dragon's freezes. The rate of change was never a property of
the language; it was a property of the speakers, and specifically of two things
about them — how many they are, and how long each one lives. Many short lives
drifting fast; one long life frozen still. This campaign made drift a function
of exactly that: sociality, and lifespan.

Then it gave the three chromatic dragons a tongue to prove it on — a harsh,
hissing Draconic, all doubled consonants and sibilants — and let the frozen rate
do its work. The result is visible in the book now, and it is quietly
remarkable: in the dictionary's derivation column, beside a dragon's every word,
where a goblin's would show a cascade of sound-changes fired, the dragon's reads
simply *no change*. The proto-word and the modern word are the same word. And
where the three goblinoid dialects have drifted measurably apart, the three
dragons — sharing one ancient proto, each frozen — have barely diverged at all:
a conservative isolate, a fossil tongue, its daughters closer to each other than
any people's are. The number is small and it is real: 0.25 against the peoples'
0.32.

Nothing of any world changed. A dragon walks no placed roster still; its tongue
lives only in the catalogue of speech, the dictionary and the phonology page,
where anyone may now hear a wyrm's name pronounced. The peoples' every word is
byte-for-byte what it was. But the eremite that only thought, last campaign, now
speaks — in a voice that has not changed in a thousand years, and will not,
because there is no one to change it with.
